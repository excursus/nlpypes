Chapter 1

e1. As Plutarch (AD 46–120) recalls in his Parallel Lives, Cato the Younger attempted to commit suicide by stabbing himself with his sword but was unable to do so because of an injured hand. His physician stitched up his wound, but after the physician left, Cato tore out his stitches and disemboweled himself. Cato did not want to live to be pardoned by Caesar, whom he considered a tyrant and with whose philosophy he violently disagreed.

 

e2. Prominent families. Melville’s mother, Maria Gansevoort Melvill (the final e was added later), was a descendant of Kiliaen van Rensselaer (c. 1580–1643), a Dutch merchant heavily involved in colonial American trade. The Randolphs were a prominent Virginia political family; the first Randolph came to colonial America in 1643. Hardicanute (1018–1042) was an unpopular Danish ruler of England.

 

e3. A reference to Adam and Eve in the book of Genesis.

 

e4. Grand Contested Election for the Presidency of the United States…. Bloody Battle in Affghanistan: The prescience of these headlines was noted in 2004, after the United States had invaded Afghanistan following the terrorist attacks of September 11, 2001, and during the contested presidential election between Al Gore and George W. Bush.

Chapter 2

e1. Like the “young candidates” of whom he writes, Melville himself had embarked from New Bedford aboard the whaleship Acushnet on January 3, 1841. He visited Nantucket for the first time in 1852, after the publication of Moby-Dick.

 

e2. A grapnel is a tool consisting of several hooks for grasping and holding; it is often attached to the end of a rope and thrown into the water to recover lost items. To sound is to dive to the bottom. Ishmael creates the image of his fingers as the hooks of a grapnel thrust to the bottom of his pocket to recover any coins that might be hidden there.

 

e3. It has been speculated by Robert K. Wallace, among others, that Melville based his description of the black church with its black preacher on Frederick Douglass’s preaching at the AME (African Methodist Episcopal) Zion Church in New Bedford following his escape from slavery.

Chapter 3

e1. In Greek mythology, the Hyperboreans were a mythical people who lived north of the north wind; in other words, in a place of bitter coldness.

 

e2. In The Sign of the Cannibal: Melville and the Making of a Postcolonial Reader (1998), Geoffrey Sanborn writes of embalmed New Zealand heads that “head peddling was a white man’s business” (130; emphasis in original). New Zealand Maori had originally embalmed the heads of the dead as memorial objects, but when a desire for the heads as curios grew among white traders, there was a great increase in warfare among Maoris in order to acquire heads that could be embalmed and sold.

 

e3. Queequeg’s tattoos and hairstyle, especially his “scalp-knot,” are very close to those of the Marquesans, with whom Melville spent the month of July 9 through August 9, 1842.

Chapter 5

e1. Fooling, teasing, playing with. Sailors usually use the term “skylark ” to mean to frolic or play, a time when they can scamper about the rigging for fun.

 

e2. John Ledyard (1751–89), an American explorer born in Groton, Connecticut, served on Captain James Cook’s third and final voyage. After his return, Thomas Jefferson suggested that Ledyard explore the American continent by traveling overland through Russia, traversing the Bering Strait, heading south through Alaska, and then crossing the American continent to Virginia. He left London in December 1786 and made it most of the way across Russia before being arrested and eventually deported to Poland. Mungo Park (1771–1806), a Scottish explorer of the African continent, was credited with being the first westerner to encounter the Niger river. See also “Niger’s unknown source” at chapter 116, e1.

e2. A line of shoals in the English Channel, near the entrance to the Strait of Dover, on which as many as two thousand ships are believed to have been wrecked.

Chapter 9

e1. A long line with a weight at the end used to measure the sea’s depth. Melville uses the verb “sound” to mean both measure and reach the bottom of.

 

e2. The “main-truck ” is the point at the top of the tallest mast, the mainmast; and the “kelson,” more commonly keelson, is a timber that binds the frames to the keel; it therefore occurs at the lowest point of the hull of a ship. Melville is quite correct that the main-truck is much higher than the keelson is low. In a whaleship the size of the Acushnet, the main-truck could be over one hundred feet in the air, whereas the keelson would lie twelve to fifteen feet below sea level.

Chapter 22

e1. Whaleships often sailed from New England ports in the middle of winter, especially the months of December and January, in order to arrive at Cape Horn at the southern tip of South America during the best months for rounding that storm-beset headland. Melville’s own ship, the Acushnet, departed on January 3, 1841. Interestingly, although Melville himself never rounded the Cape of Good Hope at the southern tip of Africa, he has the Pequod round it in Moby-Dick. Because of this change of direction, the Pequod arrives too early in the Pacific Ocean for the “Season-on-the-Line,” the ideal time to hunt whales along the Equator, and therefore heads to whaling grounds near Japan, which whalemen called the “Japan Grounds.”

 

e2. A squalid street in Liverpool, England; “the girls in Booble Alley ” are prostitutes. The chantey, or sailor’s work song, entitled “Haul Away, Joe ” includes the phrase “girls in Booble Alley. ”

Chapter 24

e1. The Egyptian sun goddess Nut was the mother of Osiris and of Isis, who was both Osiris’s sister and his wife. Osiris and Isis fell in love and mated while still in Nut’s womb.

 

e2. Melville is absolutely correct. When Commodore Matthew Perry arrived in Japan in 1853, for what has become known as the “opening of Japan,” he was able to speak with the Japanese only because John Manjiro could translate between English and Japanese. In 1841, when Manjiro was fourteen, the fishing vessel on which he was serving was wrecked, and Manjiro was rescued by an American whaleship. The whaleship’s captain carried him to Fairhaven, Massachusetts, where he received an American education and learned to speak English. He returned home to his mother in 1852, just before Perry’s arrival. Except for a single Dutch outpost, Japan had been closed to the outside world for two hundred years.

Chapter 27

e1. Tashtego is a Wampanoag Indian from the community now called Aquinnah on the western end of the island of Martha’s Vineyard. Wampanoags were the first whalemen of Nantucket and Martha’s Vineyard. After European settlement of the islands, whales were often hunted in small boats launched from shore and manned by a white officer and a crew of Wampanoags.

Chapter 32

e1. Charing Cross, often considered the center of London, England, occurs at the juncture of the Strand, Whitehall, and Cockspur Street. It was a site at which proclamations were traditionally read.

 

e2. Devilish smile; Mephistopheles is the devil to whom Faust sells his soul in the Faust legend.

Chapter 34

e1. Dough-Boy is the “progeny of a bankrupt baker and a hospital nurse” (164.37–38); hence, he is the offspring of white and white. In the nineteenth century, bankruptcy was often portrayed as bloodlessness. Dough-Boy’s father is both a bankrupt and a baker: he is bloodless, dressed in white, and covered with white flour. His mother is a hospital nurse, so she, too, dresses all in white. Melville contrasts this whiteness with the blackness of the only other boy on board the Pequod, little Pip.

 

e2. A carline is a wood stringer used as a support for hatches and cabin structures.

Chapter 35

e1. King Louis Philippe (1773–1850) ruled France from 1830 to 1848. Louis Blanc (1811–82) was a French politician and historian. Louis the Devil is a satiric reference to Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte (1808–73), who reigned as president of France and then as the emperor Napoléon III.

 

e2. The mast of a whaleship consists of three separate overlapping spars: the lower mast, top-mast, and topgallant mast. The “t’gallant cross-trees” occur at the spot where the top-mast and topgallant mast overlap high above the deck of a whaleship. A whaleman stands on the cross-trees and holds tight to the topgallant mast while looking out for whales. The precariousness of this position was made safer in the second half of the nineteenth century, when mast hoops, which encircled the whalemen, were added. Crow’s nests were rarely found on American whaleships outside of the Arctic.

Chapter 40

e1. A hogshead, like a barrel, was a cask containing a specified number of gallons; the number of gallons varied depending on the item contained within the cask. A barrel of whale oil measured 31 gallons; a hogshead measured two barrels or 63 gallons. Whale oil was usually measured in barrels, not gallons. The second Nantucket sailor’s “hogshead mouth ” would be a very large mouth, eminently suitable for loudly calling the watch. See also “tierce” at chapter 110, e1.

 

e2. A series of commands issued by the mate, which confirm the seriousness of the approaching squall. The men, or hands, are called to the halyards in order to lower the sails. The topgallant sails, the third set of square sails up the mast, are to be furled, or put away; and the topsails, the second set of square sails up the mast, are to be reefed, or made smaller.

Chapter 41

e1. The Serra da Estrela, which translates into English as “Mountain Range of the Star,” is the highest mountain range in Portugal and includes mainland Portugal’s highest peak. The “wonderful story of the Arethusa fountain” refers to a legend found in book V of the Metamorphoses of Ovid (43 BC–AD 18). Arethusa, while bathing in the Alpheus River, was seen and pursued by the river-god Alpheus in human form. Arethusa was in the retinue of Artemis, who changed her into a spring that, flowing underground, emerged at Ortygia near Syracuse.

 

e2. A knife with a six-inch blade is kept near the bow of a whaleboat in order to cut the whale line if needed. The whale line is far more dangerous than any other piece of equipment in a whaleboat. Ahab grabs the “line-knife” from the broken bow of his whaleboat and attempts to stab Moby Dick with it.

Chapter 57

e1. A kedge is a smaller anchor that is carried away from the ship in a boat and dropped to the bottom; the ship is then pulled up to the kedge. The kedge is again carried away and dropped; the ship is again pulled to the kedge. By this method, a ship can be maneuvered from one site to another in a confined space or when the wind is not favorable. A crippled beggar, like a ship in a confined or windless space, cannot move easily from one spot to another.

e1. In this long sentence, which spans almost the entire paragraph, Melville uses the image of the shark as cannibal, tying the sharks’ devouring of sailors’ corpses during a sea fight to the amputating of sailor’s limbs and other brutal medical practices of the ships’ surgeons, as well as to slave ships, where slavers metaphorically devour those whom they have enslaved. Chapter 104

e1. As he did in chapter 32, “Cetology,” Melville here plays with bibliographic terms that distinguish the sizes of books. An imperial folio is the largest printed book, an appropriate size in which to discuss the largest of all mammals. Two pages are printed on each sheet of paper for a folio, and four pages for a quarto. Melville states that he has consulted a “ huge quarto edition ” of Johnson’s A Dictionary of the English Language (1755) in his research because the lexicographer Samuel Johnson (1709–84) was such a large man.

 

e2. According to Genesis 5:25–27, Methuselah lived 969 years; he is the oldest person whose age is given in the Bible.

Chapter 107

e1. Also called trunnels, these were often made from locust wood. Holes were drilled through the planking and frames of a whaleship, then the treenails were forced into place and wedged at both ends. Treenails were cheaper than iron bolts, but more importantly they did not cause “iron sickness,” the weakening of the wood around an iron bolt that compromised the strength of a ship’s hull.

Chapter 116

e1. Finding the source of the West African river Niger consumed European explorers throughout the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. The river is very unusual in that its source is only 150 miles inland from the Atlantic Ocean, but from there the river heads into the Sahara desert; at the ancient city of Tombouctou, it turns and heads back to the ocean at the Gulf of Guinea.

Chapter 125

e1. The “log and line ” is an instrument used to measure ship’s speed. The log, which can take various forms, is dropped into the water, and the line is then allowed to run out for a specified period of time. Knots are evenly spaced along the line; by counting those knots, the ship’s speed can be determined. Speed is given in “knots,” or nautical miles per hour. A nautical mile is equal to one minute of latitude, or roughly six thousand feet.

Chapter 126

e1. A Turk’s head is a fancy knot used as a stopper knot at the end of a line. It resembles a turban, hence the name Turk’s head.

Chapter 127

e1. When the Carpenter tells Ahab that the caulking mallet is full of music, he is correct. Caulkers knew by the sound when they had driven the cotton batting and oakum used to caulk a ship far enough into the seams between the hull or deck planking. Later, the Carpenter says, “This wooden mallet is the cork, and I’m the professor of musical glasses,” tying the music made by the caulking mallet to the music produced on a glass armonica, the musical instrument invented by Benjamin Franklin.

Chapter 135

e1. The order given to move the tiller toward the upper, or windward, side of a vessel; such a movement will cause the vessel to turn away from the wind—or, in this case, away from the whale. Owen Chase, the first mate of the whaleship Essex, gave such a command to the helmsman when the Essex was struck by an eighty-foot sperm whale in 1820. Chase’s Narrative of the Most Extraordinary and Distressing Shipwreck of the Whale-Ship Essex (1821) was Melville’s most important written source for the ending of Moby-Dick.
