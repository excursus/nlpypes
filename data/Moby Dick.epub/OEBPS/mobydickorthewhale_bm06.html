Chronology


1819

Herman Melville is born on August 1 to Allan Melvill and Maria Gansevoort Melvill in New York City. He is the third of their eight children (Gansevoort, Helen Maria, Herman, Augusta, Allan, Catherine, Frances Priscilla, and Thomas) and their second son.

1830

Deeply in debt, Melville’s father, Allan Melvill, moves his family to Albany, New York. Herman and his older brother Gansevoort enter the Albany Academy.

1832

Allan Melvill dies on January 28. Gansevoort and Herman are withdrawn from the Albany Academy, thus ending the author’s formal education. Gansevoort, just sixteen, begins work for the fur company that employed his father. The final e is added to the family name.

1833

Melville spends his vacation with his uncle Thomas Melvill in Pittsfield, Massachusetts.

1837

Gansevoort Melville’s fur business fails. Melville takes a teaching post in a Pittsfield, Massachusetts, school.

1838

To escape the pressure of creditors, Maria Melville moves her family to Lansingburgh, New York. Melville takes a surveying and engineering course at the Lansingburgh Academy.

1839

Melville’s uncle Peter Gansevoort tries to find him a position in the engineering department of the Erie Canal, but without success. Melville then sails to Liverpool and back aboard the merchant vessel St. Lawrence.

1840

Melville and his friend Eli James Murdock Fly travel to Galena, Illinois, to visit his uncle Thomas Melvill, who moved to Illinois from Pittsfield, Massachusetts.

1841

Melville departs New Bedford, Massachusetts, on January 3 aboard the Fairhaven whaleship Acushnet, under the command of Captain Valentine Pease Jr. The Acushnet stops at Rio de Janeiro before rounding Cape Horn, stopping along the South American coast for wood and water, and cruising the Galápagos Islands in search of sperm whales.

1842

Rear Admiral Abel Aubert Dupetit-Thouars, aboard the French warship la Reine Blanche, takes possession of the Marquesas Islands. As part of French Polynesia, the Marquesas Islands still remain under French control.

 

On July 9, Melville and Richard Tobias Greene desert the Acushnet at Taioha’e Bay, Nuku Hiva, in the Marquesas Islands. Greene eventually leaves Nuku Hiva aboard the Fairhaven whaleship London Packet. On August 9, Melville signs aboard the Sydney, Australia, whaleship Lucy Ann, under the command of Henry Ventom. On September 24, ten members of the crew of the Lucy Ann refuse duty and are imprisoned aboard a French warship; when they are taken ashore for incarceration in a Tahitian jail, Melville joins them. Soon after October 19, he and John B. Troy escape to the neighboring island of Eimeo (now called Mooréa). In November, Melville signs aboard his third whaler, the Nantucket whaleship Charles and Henry, commanded by John B. Coleman.

1843

In May, Melville is discharged from the Charles and Henry in Lahaina, Maui, and makes his way to Honolulu, Oahu. He signs aboard the frigate United States on August 17 as an ordinary seaman.

1844

Melville is discharged from the United States Navy in Boston on October 14 and joins his family in Lansingburgh, New York.

1846

John Murray publishes the British edition of Melville’s first book under the title Narrative of a Four Months’ Residence among the Natives of a Valley of the Marquesas Islands on February 27. John Wiley, of Wiley & Putnam, publishes the American edition under the title Typee: A Peep at Polynesian Life on March 17. Melville’s brother Gansevoort dies in London on May 12. In July, Richard Tobias Greene, whom Melville has not seen since he departed Nuku Hiva in 1842, writes to the Buffalo Commercial Advertiser that he is the “true and veritable ‘Toby,’ yet living ” he and Melville meet, and Melville then composes and publishes “The Story of Toby.”

1847

Melville’s second book, Omoo, is published. On August 4, Melville marries Elizabeth Shaw, daughter of Lemuel Shaw, the chief justice of Massachusetts. Melville, his wife Elizabeth, his brother Allan, Allan’s wife Sophia, and Melville’s mother and four sisters settle together in a house in New York City.

1849

Melville’s first child, a son named Malcolm, is born on February 16. Melville’s third book, Mardi, and fourth book, Redburn, are published. Melville travels to England in October and is away from home for four months.

1850

Melville’s fifth book, White-Jacket, is published, and he begins his sixth book, Moby-Dick. On August 5, he meets Nathaniel Hawthorne. Borrowing money from his father-in-law, Melville buys a farm, which he names Arrowhead, just outside Pittsfield, Massachusetts, and moves there with his wife, son, mother, and sisters.

 

Though convinced that slavery is wrong, Judge Lemuel Shaw refuses to invalidate the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 because of his belief that application of the Constitution’s fugitive-slave clause is necessary to preserve the Union.

1851

Melville and Hawthorne continue to exchange letters and visits. Moby-Dick is published under the title The Whale by Richard Bentley in England on October 18; it is published in New York as Moby-Dick by Harper & Brothers on November 14. Melville’s second son, Stanwix, is born on October 22.

1852

With his father-in-law, Judge Lemuel Shaw, Melville visits Nantucket for the first time in July. Melville’s seventh book, Pierre, is published.

1853

Melville’s third child and first daughter, Elizabeth, is born on May 22. Melville begins contributing magazine pieces to Putnam’s and Harper’s.

1854

Melville’s eighth book, Israel Potter, is published serially in Putnam’s. This is his only full-length work to be published serially.

1855

Melville’s last child, a second daughter named Frances, is born March 2. Israel Potter is published as a book. Melville publishes the last of his magazine pieces, including the three installments of “Benito Cereno.”

1856

Five of Melville’s magazine pieces, including “ Benito Cereno ” and “Bartleby, the Scrivener ” as well as an original piece entitled “The Piazza ” are collected and published together as The Piazza Tales. Melville sails to England and then on to southern Europe and the Middle East to restore his health. Hawthorne is serving as the American consul at Liverpool; Melville visits with him at Southport.

1857

Melville continues his voyage. His last full-length fictional work, The Confidence-Man, is published.

1858

Melville’s first lecture tour. 1859 Melville’s second lecture tour.

1860

Melville’s final lecture tour ends. Melville sails on a long voyage aboard the clipper ship Meteor, captained by his youngest brother Thomas. When he reaches San Francisco, he returns home via the Isthmus of Panama.

1861

The American Civil War begins.

1863

Melville moves his family back to New York City.1865 The American Civil War ends.

1866

Harper’s publishes Battle-Pieces, Melville’s volume of Civil War poems. Melville takes the oath of office as inspector of customs at the port of New York on December 5.

1867

On September 11, Melville finds his son Malcolm dead of a self-inflicted pistol- shot.

1870

In May, Melville sits for his portrait by J. O. Eaton, which now hangs in the Houghton Library at Harvard University.

1872

Melville’s brother Allan dies February 9; his mother dies April 1.

1876

Shortly before his death, Peter Gansevoort, Melville’s uncle, pays for the publication of Melville’s long, 550-page poem, Clarel, on January 4. Melville’s sister Augusta, who often served as his amanuensis, dies on April 4.

1880

Melville’s daughter Frances marries Henry B. Thomas on April 5; she is the only one of Melville’s four children to marry.

1884

Melville’s brother Thomas dies on March 5.

1885

Melville resigns his post as customs inspector on December 31, after serving for more than nineteen years.

1886

Melville’s second son, Stanwix, dies in San Francisco.

1888

A private, limited printing of Melville’s small collection of poems, John Marr and Other Sailors, is published. 1891 Another small collection of poems, Timoleon, is privately published. Melville dies September 28; his wife and two daughters survive him. At his death, Melville leaves the unfinished manuscript of Billy Budd, Sailor.

1892

The United States Book Company publishes Moby-Dick, edited by Arthur Stedman, in the so-called deathbed edition.

1921

The Melville Revival begins with the publication of Raymond Weaver’s Herman Melville: Mariner and Mystic.

1924

Billy Budd appears for the first time in the Works of Herman Melville, Standard Edition, edited by Raymond Weaver and published in London by Constable and Company. This remains the only complete edition of Melville’s works.
