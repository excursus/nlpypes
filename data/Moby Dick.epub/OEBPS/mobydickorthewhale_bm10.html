Moby-Dick in Popular Culture

Moby Dick [was] not only ubiquitous, but immortal (for immortality is but ubiquity in time) (198)

 

An essay on Herman Melville’s Moby-Dick in popular culture could not have been written in the nineteenth century. In the forty years between the book’s publication in 1851 and Melville’s death in 1891, there were no editions other than the first English edition published by Richard Bentley and the first American edition published by Harper & Brothers. Moby-Dick languished far from public notice, unknown, uncherished, unloved. The book so permeates popular culture in the early twenty-first century that its initial fate is hard to imagine. That Melville never knew how much his masterpiece would mean to so many readers fills one with a melancholic sadness.

There is, however, one bright spot in that sadness. Melville dedicated Moby-Dick to Nathaniel Hawthorne, “In token of my admiration for his genius” (xxxv). When Hawthorne received a copy of the book, he wrote to Melville in a letter now lost. Melville’s response, however, has come down to posterity. Melville calls Hawthorne’s epistle: “your joy-giving and exultation-breeding letter” and affirms, “A sense of unspeakable security is in me this moment, on account of your having understood.” He continues:

I have written a wicked book, and feel spotless as the lamb. Ineffable socialities are in me. I would sit down and dine with you and all the gods in old Rome’s Pantheon…. You were archangel enough to despise the imperfect body, and embrace the soul.

He finally signs the letter “Herman,” but, unable to break away, adds, “I can’t stop yet” and writes a further two paragraphs (Letter of November [17?], 1851). That the person whose approbation he most desired understood what he had created in Moby-Dick was a solace to Melville himself and to the many Melville scholars that have followed since.

After Melville died in 1891, his literary executor, Arthur Stedman, supervised what has since been called the “deathbed edition ” of four of Melville’s works, including Moby-Dick (1892). Three other editions were published in the thirty years between Melville’s death and 1921. But Melville had not yet been “discovered. ”

Everything changed in 1921, the beginning of the Melville Revival. In that year, Raymond Weaver published Herman Melville: Mariner and Mystic. As biography it is lacking, for Weaver took Melville’s early writings as literally true and copied vast amounts of them into his biography. But Mariner and Mystic is extraordinarily important for the interest it aroused in Melville and his writings. Weaver went on to edit the Constable, or Standard, edition of Melville’s works, in which Moby-Dick appeared in 1922. That edition included the first appearance of Melville’s last work, Billy Budd, and it remains the only complete edition of Melville’s works.

Since 1921, Moby-Dick’s fame has continued to expand. Despite the fact that the book is as politically incorrect as possible, centering, as it does, on the killing of whales, its reputation has only increased. Even those who have never read the book recognize its basic iconography: the one-legged man in a top hat and often carrying a harpoon, the white whale, the whaleship.

The first film version of Moby-Dick was the silent black-and-white The Sea Beast, released in 1926. John Barrymore stars as Ahab (here Captain Ahab Ceeley), but the writers worried that he did not have a strong enough reason for hunting the white whale; he was therefore given an evil half brother, Derek Ceeley, and a love interest, Esther Harper, whom Derek tries to steal from Ahab. John Barrymore met Dolores Costello, who plays Esther, just before the filming of The Sea Beast and later married her; they are the grandparents of Drew Barrymore.

In 1930, a sound version was released, again starring John Barrymore but now entitled Moby Dick. Ahab now loves Faith Mapple, Father Mapple’s daughter played by Joan Bennett, but Faith must overcome her horror at his missing leg. In the film’s initial sequence, the cover of a book entitled Moby Dick slowly opens to reveal the first pages of the novel—but the screenwriter has rewritten the book’s beginning, despite putting Melville’s name on it.

That two film versions so quickly followed the beginning of the Melville Revival shows the growing popularity of Moby-Dick, but the ease with which the screenwriters changed the plot starkly points out how little the book itself was actually read. “Call me Ishmael,” the first sentence of Moby-Dick, is now so widely known that if a book entitled Moby Dick opened on screen to reveal any other sentence, it would be greeted with uproarious laughter.

As the 1920s and 1930s progressed, the literary world—writers, critics, English professors—more and more came to view Moby-Dick as a classic. Two years after Weaver’s Mariner and Mystic, D. H. Lawrence published Studies in Classic American Literature (1923), which included two chapters on Melville. Lawrence’s consideration of Melville reflects more on Lawrence himself than on his subject. For instance, he writes of Melville: “There is something curious about real blue-eyed people. They are never quite human, in the good classic sense, human as brown-eyed people are human: the human of the living humus” (chapter 10). This is certainly not literary scholarship as we understand it, but it did bring notice to Melville. Lawrence has chapters on Benjamin Franklin, J. Hector St. John de Crèvecœur, Edgar Allan Poe, Richard Henry Dana, Walt Whitman, and two chapters each on James Fenimore Cooper, Nathaniel Hawthorne, and Melville. Today all remain major figures in the American literary canon. Therefore, the fact that by 1923 Lawrence included Melville is highly significant.

Important scholars continued to write on Melville: Lewis Mumford, Herman Melville (1929); Yvor Winters, Maule’s Curse (1938); F. O. Matthiessen, American Renaissance (1941); Van Wyck Brooks, The Times of Melville and Whitman (1947); Newton Arvin, Herman Melville (1950); Lawrance Thompson, Melville’s Quarrel with God (1952); Charles Feidelson Jr., Symbolism and American Literature (1953); C. L. R. James, Mariners, Renegades and Castaways (1953); Perry Miller, The Raven and the Whale (1956); Richard Chase, The American Novel and Its Tradition (1957); Milton R. Stern, The Fine Hammered Steel of Herman Melville (1957); Leslie Fiedler, Love and Death in the American Novel (1960); H. Bruce Franklin, The Wake of the Gods: Melville’s Mythology (1963); John Seelye, Melville: The Ironic Diagram (1970); Henry Nash Smith, “The Madness of Ahab” in Democracy and the Novel (1978); Carolyn Karcher, Shadow over the Promised Land (1980); Robert K. Martin, Hero, Captain, Stranger (1986); Wai-Chee Dimock, Empire for Liberty (1989); and many more that I have left out. Black and white, male and female, straight and gay—the writers on Melville spanned all identifications.

Poets and writers, too, wrote about Melville. Two of the most important contributions were Charles Olson’s book Call Me Ishmael (1947) and Toni Morrison’s “Unspeakable Things Unspoken: The Afro-American Presence in American Literature,” published in the Michigan Quarterly Review (1989).

The Melville Revival elevated Moby-Dick to the literary canon, which led to more and more students reading the book. References to Moby-Dick then became ubiquitous. Moby-Dick entered popular culture as a result, just as Mark Twain’s Huckleberry Finn and James Fenimore Cooper’s The Deerslayer did. These books became widely familiar as they were more widely read in school. They became part of popular culture largely because they were extensively assigned as readings, and not because they were read by the general public for the sheer fun, as were Melville’s Typee, Twain’s Tom Sawyer, and Cooper’s The Last of the Mohicans.

The 1956 film Moby Dick solidified Moby-Dick’s place in popular culture. John Huston directed the film, Ray Bradbury wrote the screenplay, and Gregory Peck played the role of Ahab. Huston won four awards as best director, including awards from the Directors Guild of America and the New York Film Critics Circle. Surprisingly, during a meeting to discuss the screenplay, Bradbury informed Huston that he had “never been able to read the damned thing [Moby-Dick].”According to Sam Weller’s biography, The Bradbury Chronicles (2005), there was much tension and anger between Huston and Bradbury during the 1953–54 making of the film, allegedly due to Huston’s bullying attitude and attempts to tell Bradbury how to do his job. Bradbury’s novel Green Shadows, White Whale (1992) includes a fictionalized version of his writing the screenplay; his short story “Banshee” (1984) is another fictionalized account of what it was like to work with Huston. Mad Magazine published a satire of the movie, in which “Father Maplesyrup, an Ex-Sea Captain ” turned preacher gives a sermon on Jonah and the whale and, according to the magazine, gives away the whole plot. With the 1956 film, Moby-Dick was firmly established in both popular culture and the literary world.

Melville’s place within the literary canon, however, was a curse as well as a blessing. Moby-Dick entered the public imagination as a tremendously long and impenetrable novel. To have read Moby-Dick, or to have been forced to read Moby-Dick as a student, particularly a high school student, still elicits sympathy and compassion as often as respect. The sheer physical size of the book is played upon in the film Fried Green Tomatoes (1991). Upon being questioned about how she could have lied in court after swearing on the Bible, Ruth responds, “Well, if that judge had looked any closer, he’d have seen that it was a copy of Moby Dick.” The book is so thick, so somber, it could be mistaken for the Bible. Why would anyone read it? In the film Major League(1989), Willie Mays Hayes, looking over Jake’s shoulder, asks, “Moby Dick? What you reading that for? ” In the film Deep Impact (1998), Fish, reading to Oren Monash, who is blinded and strapped to a bunk in a spacecraft, says, “Well, let’s get started. Moby Dick. Chapter 1. ‘Call me Ishmael…’” Dr. James Wilson asks in episode 2.17, “All In,” of House M.D. (2004): “Have you read ‘Moby Dick’?” And when Macaulay Culkin goes into the library in The Pagemaster (1994), one of the books that engulfs him is, of course, Moby-Dick.

Much has also been made of the second word in the book’s title. In Matilda(1996), the child’s father, played by Danny DeVito, assumes that Moby-Dick must be trash because of its title. Matilda answers: “It’s not trash, Daddy, it’s lovely. It’s called Moby Dick by Herman Melville. ” And in Wedding Crashers (2005), Secretary Cleary says, “Well, the guy wants to run for president, he thinks Moby Dick is a venereal disease.”

Moby-Dick’s characters, too, have spawned film characters, perhaps most notably the figure of Khan in Star Trek II: The Wrath of Khan (1982). Khan makes use of Ahab’s words in stating why he must defeat Captain Kirk: “He tasks me. He tasks me and I shall have him! I’ll chase him ’round the moons of Nibia and ’round the Antares Maelstrom and ’round Perdition’s flames before I give him up!” Just in case the viewer has missed the connection, the camera at one point pans around the inside of the downed spacecraft in which Khan lives, and there on a bookshelf stand Moby-Dick, King Lear, and Paradise Lost—the latter being two of the most important written sources for Moby-Dick.

Moby-Dick not only permeates the world of film and television; it also permeates cartoons. Gary Larson’s The Far Side often revels in Moby-Dick allusions. In one, a writer sits tearing out his hair, while sheets of paper fall from his desk: “Chapter 1 / Call me Bill,” “Chapter 1 / Call me Al,” “Chapter 1 / Call me Larry,” “Chapter 1 / Call me Warren,” “Chapter 1 / Call me Roger” (1982). In another, Moby Dick, driving a truck in what appears to be New York City, has rear-ended a car, and out of the car appears a top hat, a harpoon, and a wooden leg. Moby mutters, “Crimony! Millions of people in this city, and look who I rear-end ” (1986). In a third cartoon, entitled “Moby’s parents,” an older female whale in an apron says to an older male whale sitting in an armchair, “Henry! Look!…It’s a letter from Richard” (1986). In a fourth, a little boy crouches in front of a tank full of many small black fish and one white fish with a sign reading “ Tropical Fish 15¢. ” His mother asks, “Well, Little Ahab…Which one is it going to be? ” (n.d.). And perhaps my favorite, a man in a crow’s nest on a whaleship cries out, “ The white whale! The whiiiiiite wh…No, no…My mistake!…A black whale! A regular blaaaaaaack whale!” (1984).

Charles Schulz’s Peanuts shows his fondness for Moby-Dick. In a 1976 strip, Lucy sits writing a book report: “This is my report on Dick Moby.” Charlie Brown tells her, “That’s wrong you know.” She stares at her report for a moment, then asks, “How about Richard Moby?” In an earlier (1971) strip, Charlie Brown tells Snoopy, sitting atop his doghouse with a typewriter, “You know what Herman Melville said?…He said, ‘To produce a mighty book you must choose a mighty theme.’” Snoopy ponders for a moment and then begins typing, “The Dog. ”

The difficulty of reading Moby-Dick arises as a common theme. In 2001, Jerry Scott and Jim Borgman’s Zits comic strip ran a series on that theme. In the first strip, the teenager Jeremy walks by with an enormous white whale strapped to his back. His friend asks, “Don’t tell me you still haven’t finished Moby Dick?!” A couple of strips later, Jeremy is reading, “Call me Ishmael.” He thinks to himself, “Call me bored out of my skull,” as his mother asks, “Ooh! Moby Dick! What chapter are you on?” Finally, Jeremy dreams of himself lashed to the back of a white whale, just as Gregory Peck is at the end of the 1956 film. His father says to his mother, “This summer’s reading list seems to be taking its toll on Jeremy,” but she responds by yelling at her son, “For Pete’s sake, it’s just Moby Dick! Quit whining and start reading!” to which Jeremy responds, “Groan!” The young boy in Ray Billingsley’s comic strip Curtis also faces difficulty reading Melville’s book. His teacher asks, “And the answer is, Mr. Wilkins? ” to which he replies, “Um…I dunno, Mrs. Nelson!” He mutters, “The world is turning more and more to technology, and yet here we sit studying a stupid story about a stupid captain, and his stupid obsession with a stupid white whale!…” The boy looks up to see his teacher staring at him and asks, “OH! Did I say that out loud?” (2002). In 1993, the John McPherson strip Close to Home showed a female student holding a pencil and paper: “Page 1: 1st Draft: An Analysis of Moby Dick Herman 1 Melville’s 2 novel 3 Moby 4 Dick 5 takes 6 the 7 reader 8 on 9 a 10 wild 11 sea 12 adventure 13. Basically 14, it’s 15 about 16 this 17 guy 18, Ahab 19, who 20 is 21 really 22 mad 23 at 24 this 25 whale 26. ” The girl thinks to herself, “Shoot. 1,974 words to go!” and the caption reads: “Let’s face it. Everybody does this when they’re assigned a 2,000-word term paper. ” Finally, in 1995, Bill Amend’s Fox Trot featured a whole series on Moby-Dick. In one strip, Peter asks, “Mom, which would be the better way to write a book report…to spend a little time reading the book and almost no time writing the essay…or to spend a little time on the essay and almost no time reading the book?” His mother replies, “How about putting in lots of time on each? ” to which Peter answers, “Let me clarify: a book report due in 2 hours.” His mother looks at the book and gasps, incredulous, “Moby Dick?!”

One of the best of the Moby-Dick cartoons appeared in the Wilson Library Bulletin in December 1971. A whaleman, harpoon in hand, points to a white whale in the distance, but his captain tells him, “Let it go. I don’t want to get mixed up in any damn allegory. ”

What all these cartoons and comic strips have in common is the assumption that the reader will recognize the title Moby-Dick, will understand that the book is at its most basic a story of conflict between a man with a peg leg and the white whale who bit it off, will know that the book is very long, and will be familiar with the first line, “Call me Ishmael.”

In Moby-Dick, Melville includes three chapters on artistic depictions of whales. He entitled the third: “Of Whales in Paint; in Teeth; in Wood; in Sheet-Iron; in Stone; in Mountains; in Stars” (294). Moby-Dick in popular culture could be illustrated the same way: Moby-Dick in books, in film, in television, in cartoons—and now in kitsch, in Christmas ornaments, on businesses. Moby-Dick kitsch abounds. The November 21, 1988, issue of the New Yorker included a Gahan Wilson cartoon that pokes fun at this ubiquity. Melville walks down a city street with his wife and child. His wife wears a white whale hat, white whale slippers, and a white whale scarf and ear-rings. The baby sits in a white whale shaped carriage with a white whale cap holding a white whale stuffed animal. A small boy in a similar cap tugs a white whale pull toy. A woman walks by with a white whale shaped purse and white whale glasses while in the background a man in yet another white whale cap eats a whale-shaped fish burger. The caption reads: “Herman Melville Wonders If His Agent Has Oversold Spinoffs from ‘Moby Dick.’” White whale Christmas ornaments come in all shapes and sizes and are made from every kind of material, including scallop and spider crab shells. And the name Moby Dick proudly adorns many businesses, most especially—as one might imagine—fish restaurants and take-out stands.

Perhaps Melville’s ironic sense of humor would be tickled at this evidence of universality derived from his book contemplating “the universal problem of all things” (319).
