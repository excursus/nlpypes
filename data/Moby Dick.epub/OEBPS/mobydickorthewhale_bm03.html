EXPLANATORY NOTES

Herman Melville has been lucky in his scholars, and Moby-Dick, in particular, has received the minute and exacting attention of innumerable scholars over the last seventy years. The annotations that follow are relatively spare in comparison to all that is known about this novel, and they are meant merely to assist the reader in reading (and enjoying!) the text.

For those who wish ampler annotations of passages and allusions, the notes to the Hendricks House edition of Moby-Dick (1952), edited by Luther S. Mansfield and Howard P. Vincent, are especially full. The Bobbs-Merrill edition (1964), annotated by Charles Feidelson, is also very good. The Norton edition of Moby-Dick (1967), edited by Harrison Hayford and Hershel Parker, prints a glossary of nautical terms, background and source material, and pertinent letters and contemporary reviews. The authoritative, CEAA-approved text of Moby-Dick (1988), published by the Northwestern University Press and the Newberry Library and edited by Harrison Hayford, Hershel Parker, and G. Thomas Tanselle, is reproduced in this Penguin edition. The Northwestern-Newberry edition has a full discussion of textual variants and a long and very dense historical note that includes a full discussion of the circumstances of the composition and the reception of the novel. Jay Leyda’s two-volume The Melville Log: A Documentary Life of Herman Melville 1819–1891 (1951 and 1969) remains an invaluable resource of information about Melville and his circumstances, and it is soon to appear in an expanded edition completed by Hershel Parker.

The reader with a more specialized interest in Moby-Dick or other Melville works may find one or another of the following books helpful: Kathleen E. Kier, A Melville Encyclopedia: The Novels (1990); Jill B. Gidmark, Melville Sea Dictionary (1982); Stuart M. Frank, Herman Melville’s Picture Gallery: Sources and Types of the “Pictorial” Chapters of Moby-Dick (1986); Mary Bercaw, Melville’s Sources (1987); Gail H. Coffler, Melville’s Classical Allusions: A Comprehensive Index and Glossary (1985); and Nathalia Wright, Melville’s Use of the Bible (1949). I have benefited from John Bryant’s “Texts and Discourse: The New Moby-Dick” in Resources for American Literary Study (1992) in making comments on important textual decisions made by the editors of the Northwestern-Newberry text of the novel. Eric Partridge’s Dictionary of Slang and Unconventional English (1961) may also prove useful to those interested in Melville’s wordplay. Unless otherwise indicated, the definitions of slang expressions below are derived from Partridge.


Extracts:The basic purpose of these eighty-one “Extracts” is to charge this narrative of whales and whaling with epic grandeur and significance. They are presented, more or less, in chronological order; the bibliographical information concerning both the sources of the Extracts and Etymology passages as well as Melville’s departures from the originals are discussed by the editors of the Northwestern/Newberry edition, pp. 813–830. Three of the extracts, it should be noted, have less to do with whales or whaling than with mutiny. One is the newspaper account of the “taking and retaking” of the ship Hobomock by “savages” (though Mansfield and Vincent point out that Melville must have confused the whaler Sharon, upon which such an insurrection did take place in 1842, with the Hobomock). The other two extracts are taken from William Lay and Cyrus Hussey’s A Narrative of the Mutiny on Board the Ship Globe (1828) and William Comstock’s The Life of Samuel Comstock, the Bloody Mutineer (1845). Both deal with an insurrection aboard the whaler Globe in 1824, a bloody mutiny led by an unquestionably barbarous and possibly insane Samuel Comstock, a ship’s officer, who killed the captain with an ax and became the ringleader of the revolt.

sea-gudgeon: Bait fish.

sovereignest thing on earth is parmacetti: Spermacetti; the fatty substance in the head of the sperm whale used for candles, cosmetics, and ointments.

Nescio quid sit: “I do not know what it is.”

Call me Ishmael: The biblical Ishmael was the son of Abraham by his Egyptian servant Hagar. It is prophesied of him that “he will be a wild man; his hand will be against every man, and every man’s hand against him” (Gen. 16:12). The name was commonly associated with the figure of the exile. Of the several instances where this name appears, it is typically in the form of self-address. Only once is Ishmael called by that name; Captain Peleg refers to him as Ishmael in Chapter 16. Perhaps it is worth noting that Peter Coffin calls the narrator “Skrimshander” in Chapter 3, suggestive of his function as artist and yarn-spinner.

hypos: Melancholy; an abbreviation of hypochondria. The Greek hupokhondria signifies the belly or abdomen, the seat of melancholy. Ishmael’s self-described motivation and temperament in this opening chapter mark him as a melancholic. He remarks in Chapter 35 that “nowadays, the whale-fishery furnishes an asylum for many romantic, melancholy, and absent-minded young men, disgusted with the carking cares of earth, and seeking sentiment in tar and blubber.”

city of the Manhattoes: Manhattan Island, in New York.

Pythagorean maxim: Pythagoreans followed the teachings of Pythagoras in order to purify and redeem the soul; among their strict observances was the avoidance of beans, which cause flatulence.

Tophet: Hell.

blackness of darkness: Presumably the preacher’s sermon is about false teachers, described in the General Epistle of Jude as clouds without water, trees without fruit, “raging waves of the sea, foaming out their own shame; wandering stars, to whom is reserved the blackness of darkness for ever” (Jude 12–13).

Euroclydon: The northeast wind that shipwrecked Saint Paul (Acts 27:14).

hob: A shelf in or on the side of a fireplace for keeping things warm.

skrimshander: Or scrimshaw; bone or ivory elaborately and intricately carved with designs or pictures. Melville describes the process in Chapter 57.

two dismal tallow candles, each in a winding sheet: That is, the candles are in shrouds of melted tallow.

Bulkington: Given this affectionate and heroic description, one may speculate that Bulkington was perhaps originally intended to play a larger role in the narrative than he in fact does. The novel changed its direction more than once while Melville was composing it, and this Virginian may have been designed to be a close companion to Ishmael. Also, though in a letter he himself had described his novel as merely “a romance of adventure,” there is a possibility that Melville had at one time envisioned a mutiny or some lesser confrontation aboard the Pequod. If so, Bulkington, by stature and temperament, was outfitted for such a role. He is not mentioned again, however, until Chapter 23, “The Lee Shore,” where he is permanently dismissed from the book.

brown study: A state of melancholic contemplation or perplexity.

Mt. Hecla: Presumably Mount Hekla, a volcano in Iceland.

’balmed New Zealand heads: Readers sometimes mistake these for shrunken heads, perhaps because Peter Coffin says that Queequeg with his four heads on a string looks like someone with a “string of inions.” However, the heads were embalmed, not shrunken, and life size. In fact, we are told in Chapter 13 that the last, “broken” head is sold to a barber for a block, upon which the barber will prepare wigs.

I’ll give ye a glim: A glim is an eye; that is, “I’ll give you a peek or a glimpse.”

I vum: I swear or vow.

South American ponchos: This mysterious “poncho” (as John Bryant has suggested to me) is more than likely the same sort of garment prepared for the mincers described in Chapter 95, “The Cassock.” In other words, Queequeg’s poncho is made from the skin of the whale’s penis; this may help to explain Ishmael’s reaction to his image in the mirror and his sudden anxiousness to get out of the thing.

Herr Alexander: German magician who performed in New York City in the late 1840s.

a lofty Ehrenbreitstein: Like the fortified city of Quebec, high above the St. Lawrence River on a bluff, alluded to in the previous paragraph, Ehrenbreitstein is an elevated fortification on the Rhine.

his head was phrenologically an excellent one: Phrenology was the popular pseudo-science (or “semi-science,” as Melville will later call it) of studying character according to the conformation of the skull. Melville may have been indulging in a joke at the expense of phrenologists when he wrote that “Queequeg was George Washington cannibalistically considered.” Phrenology books typically compared the heads of famous male Caucasians to women and the “lesser races,” to the benefit of white males. Thus, Africans, for example, were often diagnosed as strong in animalistic qualities but deficient in “Reason” Native Americans were characterized by “Secretiveness” women were typically seen as strong in the domestic affections such as “Amativeness” or “Ideality” but weak in “Individuality.” Few phrenologists, if any, were apt to compare favorably the head of a South Sea islander to George Washington. Melville comically applies this pseudo-science, along with its near relative physiognomy, to the face and skull of the whale in chapters 80 and 81.

like Czar Peter content to toil in the shipyards: In 1698, Peter the Great worked as a common laborer in a naval yard in England in order to learn the art of shipbuilding.

spile: A wooden plug or bung.

the three pirate powers: Prussia, Russia, and Austria partitioned Poland in the late eighteenth century.

opened a white church: Spotted a white church.

XXXIX Articles: Articles of faith of the Church of England.

Pequod: Or Pequot. Actually a Connecticut, not a Massachusetts, tribe, whose village was burned and the inhabitants slaughtered in 1637. The few Pequots that remained mingled with other New England tribes, so they may as well have been as “extinct as the ancient Medes,” as Melville says. The name is appropriate, for the description of the ship and the prophecies that accumulate around the fated voyage mark the Pequod as a death ship.

three old kings of Cologne: The magi, whose remains were supposedly buried in the Cathedral of Cologne and whose skulls were publicly exhibited.

Thorkill-Hake: Eleventh-century Icelandic hero, whose adventures were illustrated on his carved bedstead.

she sported there a tiller: Later (chapters 61 and 118), the “spokes” of the turnstile wheel the Pequod had apparently scorned are referred to. Evidently, this reference is a survival of some earlier conception of the ship.

Captain Peleg…[and]Bildad: Peleg and Bildad are biblical names, the latter the sanctimonious comforter of Job. Melville’s Bildad is equally righteous, but his captains are a humorous pair. Surely one of the earliest versions of the “good cop and bad cop” confidence game, their pretended feud conspires to give Ishmael the 300th lay when he had hoped for a much handsomer share.

stiver: A Dutch coin.

Ahab of old, thou knowest, was a crowned king: The biblical story of Ahab appears in I Kings 16–22. Most significant about the name is that Ahab was an idolator who “did evil in the sight of the Lord” and did “provoke” the Lord more than any king before him. Melville’s Ahab is fifty-eight years old and first went to sea when he was eighteen. He is a man who is “desperate moody, and savage,” and all bent on revenge. But Melville at least mollifies the biblical association by having Peleg inform Ishmael that Ahab has his “humanities,” that he is fairly recently married to a woman much younger than he and that they have a child.

castor: A small vial or cruet for condiments.

pilau: Or pilaf; a steamed rice dish, typically with meat or shellfish.

Quohog: Peleg substitutes the name of a familiar thick-shelled clam for the more difficult Queequeg.

Quohog, his X mark: John Bryant informs me that South Sea islanders typically reproduced a portion of their facial tattoos as a signature. Here Queequeg reproduces a “queer round figure” tattooed on his arm. In any event, typographical necessity rendered Queequeg’s mark as an X.

for fear of after-claps: Unexpected after-effects or sequels.

the silver calabash he spat into: Presumably the vestige of an earlier conception of Ahab’s impiety; the reference is never explained.

Elijah: The biblical Elijah had prophesied Ahab’s destruction (I Kings 19–23). Ishmael dismisses the man’s forebodings as “humbug” at the end of this chapter, but in Chapter 28, “Ahab,” his anxiousness about his captain is heightened by “the ragged Elijah’s diabolical incoherences uninvitingly recurring to me, with a subtle energy.”

a small choice copy of Watts: That is, a hymnal. Isaac Watts (1674–1748) was the author of hymns and some versions of the psalms. Bildad sings a Watts hymn as he pilots the Pequod to sea.

my first kick: The use of the word “first” is puzzling, for there are no future kicks. The editors of the Northwestern/Newberry edition speculate that Peleg, not Ahab, was originally conceived to be the captain of the Pequod and that further blows from him would be in keeping with his rough character. If in fact the two captains were about to separate for the length of the voyage, as they further observe, this may also help to explain why Bildad grasps Peleg’s hand and stands “gazing” into his face. This abusive “poke” may at one time have presaged further abuses and some sort of retaliation from the crew.

thou sheep-head: A talkative person; a chatterbox.

Cook or Vancouver: James Cook (1728–1779) and George Vancouver (1758–1798), English explorers.

Krusenstern: Adam Johann van Krusenstern (1770–1846), a Russian explorer.

See subsequent chapters for something more on this head: Meville’s note, referring to chapters 82 and 90.

his old rigadig tunes: Possibly rigadoon; rapid, double-metered tunes.

an Ahasuerus to behold: Biblical king of Persia who reigned from India “even unto Ethiopia” (Esther 1:1)

An Anacharsis Clootz deputation: In 1790 the German nobleman Jean Baptiste Clootz appeared with a gathered deputation of foreigners before the French National Assembly to demonstrate the universal endorsement of the French Revolution.

Black little Pip—he never did: In the American edition, after this passage the phrase “oh, no! he went before” appeared. It was canceled in the English edition, but at some point in the composition of the novel Melville evidently foresaw Pip’s death before the sinking of the Pequod. Pip does go mad, but he does not die before the rest. Nevertheless, the tone and quality of Ishmael’s lamentation in response to Pip’s untimely death is evident even here.

“He has a quiver of ’em”: Another discrepancy, since Ahab would not have had to instruct the carpenter to make him a new pegleg, as he does in Chapter 106, if he had a quiver of extras in his cabin.

Enter Ahab; to him, Stubb: This is the first instance of dramatic technique that will become more common (for example, chapters 37–39, 70–72, and 77), as the narrative acquires and emphasizes its dramatic and particularly Shakespearean qualities.

Tic-Dolly-row: Or tic douloureux; literally a sad or painful twitching, here indicative of a troubled conscience.

’Slid!: God’s lid; an oath.

Many are the men: Of this list of whaling “authorities,” the ones who proved most useful to Melville and from whose writings he borrowed most heavily are Thomas Beale, The Natural History of the Sperm Whale (1839); J. Ross Browne, Etchings of a Whaling Cruise (1846); William Scoresby, Jr., An Account of the Arctic Regions, with a History and Description of the Northern Whale-Fishery (1820). For an extensive treatment of Melville’s use of whaling sources, see Howard Vincent’s The Trying-Out of Moby-Dick (1949) or the notes to the Hendricks House edition of Moby-Dick.

warm bilocular heart: Heart having two chambers.

penem intrantem feminam mammis lactantem: “A penis which enters the female who gives milk from her breasts.”

jure meritque: “Justly and deservedly from the law of nature.”

Duodecimo Whale: These are printer’s terms for the size of books, the folio being the largest, the duodecimo the smallest. Melville omits the quarto from his taxonomy for reasons stated later in the chapter.

He is, without doubt, the largest inhabitant of the globe: Actually the blue or “sulphur bottom” whale is considerably larger than the sperm whale. Whether or not Melville knew this is uncertain; Ishmael later says that he has seen this whale at too “great a distance” to study him. In any event, the grand scale of this epic quest required that the sperm whale Moby Dick be deemed the largest creature in the seas.

gnomon-like fin: That is, the fin is like the perpendicular post on a sundial.

Ahaz-dial the shadow often goes back: “Behold, I will bring again the shadow of the degrees which is gone down in the sun dial of Ahaz, ten degrees backward” (Isa. 38:8), Because the whale does not remain stationary, the shadow cast by his fin is erratic.

with rope-walks of line: Long, narrow alleyways where ropes are made.

Black Letter tells me: That is, Richard Hakluyt’s Principal Navigations (1598).

the Earl of Leicester…did likewise present her highness with another horn, pertaining to a land beast: Likely a bawdy joke, since the Earl of Leicester was one of the Queen’s favorites.

in terrorem: To instill fear in them.

binnacle: The stand in which the ship’s compass sits.

To have been Belshazzar, King of Babylon: “Belshazzar the king made a great feast to a thousand of his lords, and drank wine before the thousand” (Dan. 5:1).

that wild Logan of the woods: John Logan, a defiant Shawnee leader who, intent on revenge for the massacre of his family by whites, refused to participate in peace talks.

Captain Sleet: A parodic reference to William Scoresby Jr., whose father had evidently invented the crow’s nest and from whose An Account of the Artic Regions Melville had borrowed heavily in writing Moby-Dick.

instead of Bowditch: Nathaniel Bowditch (1773–1838), American mathematician and astronomer.

Ten thousand blubber hunters sweep over thee in vain: Melville’s hypothetical young philosopher adapts Byron’s “Ten thousand fleets sweep over thee in vain” to his own circumstance.

Wickliff’s sprinkled Pantheistic ashes: The martyred reformer William Wycliffe was disinterred and his body burned in 1428. His ashes were deposited in a brook and flowed into the ocean.

Over Descartian vortices you hover: This often cited passage has a double, perhaps a triple, significance. Most obviously it is a commentary on Cartesian dualism, for Ishmael’s reveries may be permanently interrupted by a false step. Second, the phrase refers to René Descartes’s (1596–1650) theory that all motion occurs in circular patterns and that the universe is inhabited by countless vortices. Finally, there is the residual suggestion of the “Cartesian devil diver”—a philosophical parlor toy consisting of a figure in a glass tube partly filled with water, the open end covered with elastic or rubber; as the elastic is depressed the diver sinks, and upon release the diver rises. Here Ishmael imagines a diver who sinks “no more to rise for ever.”

St. Vitus’ imp: St. Vitus’s dance, or chorea, a nervous disorder characterized by uncontrollable shaking, though Ahab’s subsequent reference to “ague” suggests the shaking is due to fever. Actually, his shaking is likely due to his own passionate hate of the whale that he has transmitted to his crew.

Iron Crown of Lombardy: Crown used at the coronation of Holy Roman Emperors.

deaf Burkes and blinded Bendigoes: Jem (“Deaf”) Burke and William Thompson (nicknamed “Bendigo”), early nineteenth-century prize-fighters.

I twigged it: I observed it.

Here’s a carcase: A carcass. The sense here is that Stubb has lost his character or substance since his dream in the “Queen Mab” chapter. He no longer has the will to rebel or to oppose Ahab.

before we ride to anchor in Blanket Bay: Before we go to bed.

Seeva: Shiva or Siva, member of the Hindu triad consisting of Brahma (the Creator), Vishnu (the Preserver), and Shiva (the Destroyer).

Moby Dick: Melville was surely aware of the legend of the white whale Mocha Dick and gave his own mythical creation a modified version of that name, though according to the tale Mocha Dick was in fact killed. Melville apparently meant from the beginning that his white whale should survive.

this monomania: Given Ishmael’s casual and familiar use of this term, it is perhaps surprising to learn that in 1851 the word was of fairly recent origin; it was coined in 1823 by the French psychiatrist Jean Esquirol. James C. Prichard in the Cyclopedia of Practical Medicine (1833) wrote that the term monomania “has been adopted of late times instead of melancholia” (OED). The Penny Cyclopedia (1843), which provided Melville with other source material for Moby-Dick, observed that along with the obsessive character of the monomaniac there is “generally combined with the delusion a morbid state of the moral feelings” (OED). Though Melville often describes Ahab as obsessive and morbid, he never uses the word melancholy in reference to him.

Roman halls of Thermes: The Hôtel de Cluny was a medieval palace in Paris; beneath it were Roman baths (thermae).

so like a Caryatid, he patiently sits: A caryatid is a sculptural supporting column, typically in the form of a female, not a male, and standing, not sitting.

Pegu: Burmese city.

whooping-imps of the Blocksburg: Blocksberg is the highest peak among the Hartz mountains in central Germany and the supposed site of an annual witches’ Sabbath.

the snow-howdahed Andes: That is, the snow sits atop the Andes like those canopied seats made for elephants or camels.

murderous savage Annawon: The Wampanoag leader Annawon was actually pursued and captured by Captain Benjamin Church in 1676. Lieutenant Colonel William Butler pursued another Indian leader in 1778.

Langsdorff’s Voyages: Georg Heinrich von Langsdorff’s Voyages and Travels in Various Parts of the World was published in 1813.

I can’t see three seas off: That is, he can’t see more than three waves off.

Fits? yes, give him fits: In nineteenth-century slang, to give one fits is to make one suffer a humiliating defeat.

the devils also, add the uncanonical Rabbins, indulged in mundane amours: In Genesis 6:1–4, the sons of God “came in unto the daughters of men, and they bare children to them.” But the authors of the uncanonical Book of Enoch and Book of Jubilees interpret this scripture to mean that devils visited the earth, and their children were unholy and destructive.

As if the waves had been fullers: Fullers are those who beat and shrink cloth during its manufacture in order to make the fabric more dense.

I shall preserve the style in which I once narrated it: “The Town-Ho’s Story,” some Melville scholars believe, is a vestige of Melville’s earlier conception of his novel; hence, as this passage hints, this is a “twice told tale.” In any event, the story continues some of the foreshadowings in earlier chapters that are never satisfied in the main narrative. Harrison Hayford has argued persuasively that the early plans for Peleg as an abusive captain of the Pequod survive in the character of Radney and that Bulkington was meant to play the defiant role played here by Steelkilt.

St. Mark!—St. Dominic: St. Mark, the patron saint of Venice; St. Dominic, founder of the Dominican order, is the patron saint of the Cathedral of Lima.

behind the barricade: That is, as Parisians behind barricades during the street fighting that occurred during the republican revolutions in nineteenth-century France.

mince ye up for the try-pots: Large pots used for rendering whale oil; this process is dramatically described in Chapter 96, “The Try-Works.”

Of the Monstrous Pictures of Whales: This and the next two chapters deal with pictorial representations of whales; Ishmael’s treatment of this subject is progressive in the sense that he deals with the most fantastic representations first and moves toward the more authentic depictions. The lavishly illustrated Herman Melville’s Picture Gallery: Sources and Types of the “Pictorial” Chapters of Moby-Dick (1986), by Stuart M. Frank, includes the original or analogous representations to which Melville refers, and it is a delightful and instructive gloss on these chapters.

I doubt not the captain had this veracious picture taken for the benefit of the marines: That is, for those gullible enough to believe it. “Marine” was often used to signify an ignorant seaman; hence the slang expression “Tell it to the marines” means tell it to someone credulous enough to believe you.

the live ground opened and swallowed them up forever: If the Lord “make a new thing, and the earth open her mouth, and swallow them up…then ye shall understand that these men have provoked the Lord” (Num. 16:30).

the great Kraken: Legendary sea creature said to exist off the coast of Norway.

the Anak of the tribe: The spies Moses sent to Canaan bring back the report that “there we saw the giants, the sons of Anak” (Num. 13:33).

the six burghers of Calais before King Edward: The six who, in 1347, voluntarily responded to King Edward III’s promise to spare Calais in exchange for the lives of six notable citizens and appeared with ropes around their necks.

Mazeppa: Byronic character who is tied to a wild horse.

lubber’s hole: Opening in the main top preferred by amateur sailors to mounting by means of the shrouds.

some young bucks…by and by get to have a little brains of their own, so as to be able to tell a calf’s head from their own heads: Another extended pun; a “calf” is slang for a brainless person.

formerly: The Northwestern-Newberry editors have changed the copytext’s “formally” to “formerly” on the grounds that it better conforms to the sense of this and the next sentence. It is possible, however, as John Bryant observes, that “formally” was in fact Melville’s intention, since it emphasizes the hypocrisy of the Secretary.

Agassiz: Louis Agassiz (1807–1873), Swiss-born naturalist and geologist who taught at Harvard after 1848.

There are other ghosts than the Cock-Lane one, and far deeper men than Doctor Johnson who believe in them: Actually Samuel Johnson (1709–84), among others, investigated the spirit rappings on Cock Lane and exposed the fraud.

from the girdle of Judith: In the Apochrypha, the Hebrew widow Judith beheads Holofernes and brings the head to her people in order to urge them into battle against the Assyrians (Judith 13).

The Jeroboam’s Story: Jeroboam, the wicked king of Israel recorded in I Kings 11–14.

Neskyeuna Shakers: Niskayuna, a village in New York and the founding place of American Shakers, a celibate, communal sect whose official name was “The United Society of Believers in Christ’s Second Coming.”

opening of the seventh vial: The seventh vial of wrath recorded in Revelation 16:17–21; when it was poured into the air it brought forth the destruction of thunder, lightning, earthquakes, and hailstorms.

Ahab’s feet: Melville’s biographer Leon Howard hypothesized that Ahab’s pegleg may have been a late addition to the novel and that this reference to Ahab’s “feet” may be a vestige of that earlier conception. Ahab is often unusually active, even nimble, for a man with one leg.

jalap: Mexican plant whose root is used as a cathartic.

gamboge: Yellowish brown.

on one side you hoist in Locke’s head…on the other side, hoist in Kant’s: John Locke (1632–1704), British empiricist philosopher; Immanuel Kant (1724–1804), German idealist philosopher.

a terrific portcullis: A sliding grill with pointed ends suspended over the gateway of a fortification, which can be lowered suddenly in case of attack.

This Right Whale I take to have been a Stoic; the Sperm Whale, a Platonian, who might have taken up Spinoza in his latter years: Though Melville has changed his allusions from Locke and Kant to Stoics, Plato, and Spinoza, this is essentially a continuation of the distinction made earlier between the practical and empiricist Right Whale and the transcendental idealist and absolutist Sperm Whale.

stove a passage through the Isthmus of Darien: The Isthmus of Panama; Melville imagines the sperm whale accomplishing an engineering feat that, in fact, will not be achieved until the completion of the Panama Canal in 1914.

the dread goddess’s veil at Sais: In Schiller’s poem “The Veiled Statue at Sais,” a youth seeking to learn of Truth lifts the veil of a statue in the temple of Athena. What he saw there is not disclosed, but he becomes an unhappy man and dies young.

Heidelburgh Tun: The enormous wine cask in Heidelberg with a capacity of nearly 50,000 gallons.

fine pelisse: An outer cloak, typically of fur or fur-lined.

sanctum sanctorum: Holy of holies; the innermost sanctuary of the Temple of Jerusalem where the Ark of the Covenant was located.

Physiognomist or Phrenologist: Physiognomy is the examination of character according to the study of faces; Johann Lavater (1741–1801) was a physiognomist. Phrenology refers to the study of character according to the conformation of the skull; Franz Joseph Gall (1758–1828) and his “disciple” Johan Spurzheim (1776–1832) were phrenologists.

Champollion deciphered the wrinkled granite hieroglyphics: Jean François Champollion (1790–1832), the French Egyptologist who deciphered the Rosetta Stone.

duff: A pudding made of flour, raisins, and water; “duff days” were Thursday and Sunday, when this mixture was served to the crew instead of meat.

Dutch dogger: An awkward Dutch vessel and, by extension, a derogatory reference to those who sail in her; similarly, “butter-boxes” (below) refers both to a Dutch craft and Dutchmen. Melville returns to this theme in Chapter 101 when he remarks on the astonishing amount of butter and cheese unctuous Dutch sailors consumed.

“Thou art as a lion of the waters, and as a dragon of the sea,” saith Ezekiel: Ezekiel 32:2.

only the stump or fishy part of him remained: “Behold, Dagon was fallen upon his face to the ground before the ark of the Lord; and the head of Dagon and both palms of his hands were cut off upon the threshold; only the stump of Dagon was left to him” (I Sam. 5:4).

Arion and the dolphin: According to legend, Arion, the Greek poet, jumped into the sea to escape robbery and was taken to land by a dolphin.

Ptolemy Philopater: Ptolemy XIII, Egyptian ruler and brother of Cleopatra.

King Juba: African king (ca. 50 B.C.–A.D. 24).

To gally, or gallow…in the New World: The editors of the Northwestern-Newberry edition of the novel have added this note, which appeared in the English edition but not the American. As Harrison Hayford points out, the style, substance, and broad humor indicate that this note is almost surely Melville’s, not some addition made by an editor for an English audience, and therefore properly belongs in an authoritative text.

more hominum: In the manner of men.

en bon point: Probably embonpoint, “corpulent.”

Bashaw: Or pasha, a Turkish title of honor.

those occult lessons he inculcated into some of his pupils: Eugène François Vidocq (1775–1857), former criminal turned detective, records seducing some of his pupils in a country school.

Coke-upon-Littleton: Name for Sir Edward Coke’s seventeenth-century commentary on Sir Thomas Littleton’s summary of fifteenth-century property law.

Republican slaves: That is, slaves in a republic, as in the United States, and perhaps a sardonic reference to the recently enacted Fugitive Slave Law (1850).

John Bull…Brother Jonathan: John Bull is England, Brother Jonathan is the United States, which annexed Texas in 1845.

What at last will Mexico be to the United States?: An interesting and, in 1851, perhaps not so farfetched conclusion to this brief survey of colonial expansion. Between 1845 and the end of the Mexican-American War and the Treaty of Guadalupe-Hidalgo in 1848, the United States acquired Texas, California, much of Colorado, Utah, most of Arizona, and New Mexico. Not until the Gadsden Purchase (1853) were the bitter boundary disputes settled and the present limits of the continental U.S. established.

“De balena vero sufficit, si rex habeat caput, et regina caudam”: “Concerning the whale, it really suffices that the king should have the head and the queen the tail.”

fobbing: Pocketing; procuring illegally.

Plowden: Edmund Plowden (1518–1585), an English jurist.

a Crappo that is content with our leavings: French crapaud, “toad.” That is, a derogatory term for the French analogous to the more familiar epithet “frogs,” but also punningly in this context, meaning one that will settle for our “leavings,” or crap.

Brandreth’s pills: Brand name for a nineteenth-century patent medicine; a laxative.

Fogo Von Slack: A comic reference to William Scoresby.

bore down upon the Turkish frigates, and folded them in conflagrations: Strategy used 1822 by Canaris during the Greek war for independence.

Man of Sorrows: Isaiah 53:3; generally accepted as a reference to Christ.

the man that wandereth…in the congregation of the dead: Proverbs 21:16

metempsychosis: Transmigration of souls. Pythagoras was a believer in this form of reincarnation; here Ishmael imagines himself two thousand years later instructing Pythagoras, now reborn as a novice sailor.

Belshazzar’s awful writing: A divine warning written on the wall. Daniel interpreted the words for the King of Babylon and was rewarded with a third of the kingdom (Dan. 5). Melville quotes those mysterious words—“Mene, Mene, Tekel, Upharsin”—in Chapter 119.

Jimini: Slang; also spelled “gemminy” or “jimminy.” An expression of surprise.

testing cruise: The Northwestern-Newberry editors have emended the phrase from the copytext’s “tasting cruise” in order to bring it into conformity with the notion of a “noble experimental cruise” mentioned in the next sentence. John Bryant, while accepting the change, speculates that “tasting” may in fact have been Melville’s original intention and meant for a pun.

anti-scorbutic: That is, a cure for or preventative of scurvy; since the “fresh fare” is probably weevils, the bread fails of its purpose.

like the hair-hung sword that so affrighted Damocles: As penalty for his overpraise of the Syracusan tyrant Dionysius, Damocles was invited to a banquet and seated beneath a sword hanging by a hair.

one of the most extraordinary creatures which the mutations of the globe have blotted out of existence: The bones found on the Alabama plantation were first identified as those of a reptile (Basilosaurus, or king lizard), but the British naturalist Sir Richard Owen identified it as an extinct species of whale. In 1845 Albert Koch assembled a very large skeleton using some of these bones along with others not from the original discovery and publicly exhibited it in New York and Boston as authentic. Evidently, the public was as credulous as were Judge Creagh’s slaves.

Thames Tunnels of Whales: In London, the 1,300-foot-long tunnel beneath the Thames was opened in 1843. In Chapter 108, Ahab employs the same figure as a comparison to the desired girth of the chest of his imagined “complete man.”

his ivory limb…all but pierced his groin: This later accident explains why Ahab kept to his cabin before and after the departure of the Pequod, and it supplies a deeper motive for revenge; for he is not only “dismasted” but apparently unmanned as well.

multum in parvo: Much in little.

you old Smut: “Smut!” or “brother smut,” a slang expression meaning “ditto” or “same to you.” The comic sense is that the carpenter, who like Ahab is given to soliloquizing, is upbraiding himself. The pairing of Ahab and the carpenter is deliberate and revealing. Both have a “subtle something” within them, and Ahab seems to recognize this, since he asks the carpenter whether he does not feel “some entire, living, thinking thing” standing where he now stands. The difference, of course, is that the carpenter’s doubled self, as opposed to Ahab’s, is benign and attributable to mere dotage.

taking altitudes on it: Double reference: using a quadrant and arrogantly indulging in elevated moods, such as Ahab has just done with the carpenter.

up Burtons and break out: Hoist the casks out of stowage.

Mother Carey’s chickens: According to Eric Partridge, a slang nautical expression for snow; here the sparks rain down on Ahab like snowflakes. But also, more conventionally, a slang expression for petrels, to which Ahab alludes when he speaks of the sparks as birds of omen.

“Ego non baptizo te in nomine patris, sed in nomine diaboli!”: “I baptize thee not in the name of the Father, but in the name of the Devil.” Melville remarked in a letter to Hawthorne (June 29, 1851) that this is the secret “motto” of his book.

“Oh, grassy glades!…we must there to learn it.”: The editors of the Northwestern-Newberry text, quite properly, have added the quotation marks to this paragraph. Neither the American or the English editions of Moby-Dick printed these quotation marks, but the context makes clear that the paragraph is spoken by Ahab, not Ishmael, as some critics have assumed.

Asphaltites: The Dead Sea.

corpusants: Luminous balls of electrical energy.

Herculaneum: City destroyed by the eruption of Vesuvius along with Pompeii; the remains of the victims in a variety of attitudes were preserved in the ashes.

Ho, gluepots: A parson, someone who “glues” people together, or marries them.

What a hooroosh: Probably “hurroosh,” cries of triumph and excitement.

I make a bandbox: A box designed to hold collars for shirts.

Aroostook: County in Maine.

Cruppered with a coffin: A crupper is a leather strap attached to a saddle and looped around a horse’s tail; here, the sense is being saddled with the job of caulking a coffin.

Middle aisle of a church! What’s here?: The sense here is, “What’s this, the middle aisle of a church, that a coffin should be in the middle of the deck?”

She was Rachel, weeping for her children, because they were not: “A voice was heard in Ramah, lamentation, and bitter weeping; Rachel weeping for her children refused to be comforted for her children because they were not” (Jer. 31:15).

base of the Eddystone: Base of the lighthouse on the Eddystone reef in the English Channel.

elephants in the book of Maccabees: “And to the end they might provoke the elephants to fight, they showed them the blood of grapes and mulberries” (I Macc. 6:34; in the Apochrypha).

one concrete hull: That is, a coalescence of these several ingredients.

Keep him nailed: Keep an eye on him.

heliotrope glance: Literally a turning toward the sun. Like a sundial, as well as the flower, his glance awaits the dawn light.

Monadnock hump: A bald, domed mountain in New Hampshire.

Fata Morgana: Named after Morgan le Fay, an enchantress of Arthurian legend, a mirage; under certain conditions, objects on the water are reflected upside down.

as it rolled five thousand years ago: Or, according to biblical chronology, at the time of the Flood.

And I only am escaped alone to tell thee: Four messengers speak to Job and tell him of the destruction and loss of his property, servants, and sons. Each concludes with the words “And I only am escaped to tell thee” (Job 1:14–19).

like another Ixion: According to Greek myth, for attempting to seduce Hera, Zeus punished Ixion by having him bound to a fiery wheel in Tartarus.
